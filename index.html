🎸 How to Identify a Guitar

Fretboard Fred's quick-reference guide. Look at these features in order:

Body Shape

Strat? Tele? Les Paul? SG? Offset? Shape tells you the family immediately.

Headstock Shape

Every brand has a signature headstock. Fender, Gibson, PRS -- the head never lies.

Pickup Types

Single coil, humbucker, P90, mini-humbucker. Configuration tells you the era and model.

Serial Numbers

On the headstock, neck plate, or inside the body. The DNA of every guitar.

Bridge Type

Tune-o-matic, tremolo, hardtail, Floyd Rose. Narrows down the model fast.

Tuner Types

Kluson, Grover, locking, vintage-style. Tuners date a guitar better than most features.

Classic Guitars -- Quick Reference

Fender Stratocaster -- Contoured double-cutaway, 3 single coils, tremolo bridge. The most copied guitar in history.
Gibson Les Paul -- Single-cutaway, carved maple top, 2 humbuckers, tune-o-matic bridge. Rock's workhorse since 1952.
Fender Telecaster -- Slab body, 2 single coils, through-body stringing. The original solid-body electric.
Gibson SG -- Double-cutaway, thin body, 2 humbuckers. Lighter than a Les Paul, plays like a dream.
PRS Custom 24 -- Double-cutaway, 24 frets, bird inlays, tremolo. Modern classic since 1985.
Martin D-28 -- Dreadnought acoustic, rosewood back and sides, spruce top. The gold standard of flat-tops.

🔧 Setup Basics

String Height (Action)

Action is the distance between strings and frets. Too high and it's hard to play. Too low and you get fret buzz. Most players want about 2mm at the 12th fret on the treble side, 2.5mm on the bass side. Measure with a ruler or action gauge.

How to adjust: On a Strat, raise or lower the saddle screws. On a Les Paul, turn the thumbwheels on the bridge posts. Small turns -- quarter turns at a time.

Truss Rod

The truss rod controls neck relief -- the slight bow in the neck. Too much bow = high action in the middle frets. Too flat = fret buzz everywhere. You want a tiny amount of relief -- about 0.2mm at the 8th fret when holding the string at frets 1 and 17.

Warning: Quarter turns only. Let the neck settle overnight between adjustments. If you're not confident, take it to a tech. Snapping a truss rod is an expensive mistake.

Intonation

If your guitar is in tune on open strings but sharp or flat at the 12th fret, your intonation is off. Compare the 12th fret note to the 12th fret harmonic. If the fretted note is sharp, move the saddle back (longer string). If flat, move it forward.

Tip: Always set intonation AFTER adjusting action and truss rod. New strings, tuned up, then intonate.

Pickup Height & Cleaning

Pickup Height: Start at 3mm from the string (last fret held) for humbuckers, 2.5mm for single coils. Too close = magnetic pull throws tuning off. Too far = weak signal.
Cleaning: Lemon oil on rosewood fretboards (NOT maple). Polish body with guitar-specific polish. Clean strings after playing to triple their life. WD-40 on rusty hardware -- but keep it off the wood.

🛡️ Spotting Fakes -- Fred's Guide

Fred's Rule: If the price seems too good to be true, it is.

Chinese copies have got scary good in the last 10 years. I've seen fakes that fooled shop owners. But there are always tells if you know where to look. Here's what I check every single time.

Logo & Headstock

-- Check the font. Fakes often get the lettering slightly wrong -- spacing, thickness, shape.

-- On Gibsons, the "i" in Gibson should NOT dot. It's an open-top "i".

-- Fender logos: check the spacing between letters, especially the "F" spaghetti logo on vintage models.

-- Look for sloppy inlay work. Real pearl inlays have natural variation. Plastic ones look too perfect.

Serial Numbers & Hardware

-- Serial numbers should be consistent with the era. Google the serial number format for the brand.

-- Cheap tuners are the biggest giveaway. Real Grovers and Klusons have weight and precision.

-- Check the bridge. Genuine tune-o-matics have clean casting. Fakes have rough edges and thin plating.

-- Weigh the guitar. Genuine Les Pauls are HEAVY (8-10 lbs). Fakes are often lighter -- cheaper wood.

The Detail Test

-- Look inside the control cavity. Real guitars have neat wiring. Fakes have messy solder and cheap pots.

-- Check the neck pocket (bolt-on guitars). It should be tight, clean, and have a date stamp or initials.

-- Fret ends: real quality guitars have smooth, rolled fret ends. Fakes have sharp, scratchy frets.

-- Smell the case. Sounds mad, but Chinese fake cases have a distinct chemical smell that takes months to fade.

🎵 Common Questions

"If it's got frets and a question mark, I'm your man." -- Fretboard Fred

How do I know if my guitar is real or fake?

+
Check the serial number first -- Google the format for your brand. Gibson serials are on the back of the headstock. Fender serials are on the headstock front, neck plate, or heel. Then look at the details: logo font, tuner quality, fret finish, binding, and the weight. Chinese copies are lighter, have rough hardware, and the inlays look too uniform. Check the neck pocket for stamps. Open the electronics cavity -- real guitars have clean wiring, fakes have cheap pots and sloppy solder. When in doubt, snap a photo and use the Guit-Oid AI above.

What's a guitar setup and do I need one?

+
A setup is adjusting the action (string height), truss rod (neck relief), intonation (tuning accuracy up the neck), and pickup height. Every guitar needs one eventually -- wood moves with temperature and humidity. A new guitar from a shop SHOULD be set up, but often isn't. If your guitar buzzes, feels hard to play, or won't stay in tune past the 5th fret, it needs a setup. A good tech charges 30-50 quid and it transforms the playability. Or learn to do it yourself -- the Learn section above has the basics.

Is my old guitar worth anything?

+
Maybe! It depends on brand, model, year, condition, and originality. A 1960s Fender or Gibson in original condition can be worth thousands. But a 1990s Squier might be worth 80 quid. Key factors: Is it all original? (Original pickups, tuners, bridge, finish). Has it been refinished? (Kills value on vintage). Does it play well? Check the serial number to date it, then search completed eBay listings for your model and year. That gives you real market value, not wishful thinking. Or photograph it and ask Fred.

What's a "Lawsuit Era" guitar?

+
In the 1970s, Japanese companies like Tokai, Greco, Burny, and Orville made copies of Fender and Gibson guitars that were so good, the American companies sued. These "Lawsuit Era" guitars (roughly 1975-1985) are now collectible in their own right. The craftsmanship was often BETTER than what Gibson and Fender were producing at the time. A Tokai Love Rock from 1980 can be as good as a real Les Paul -- and they're climbing in value. Don't dismiss a Japanese copy. It might be the best guitar you ever play.

Should I refinish my vintage guitar?

+
NO. Please, no. I beg you. A refinished vintage guitar loses 50-70% of its value overnight. That worn, checking, faded finish IS the value. It's called patina, mojo, whatever -- it's proof the guitar is real and has been loved. The only exception is if the guitar is already so damaged that the wood is at risk. Then a sympathetic, period-correct refinish by a specialist might be justified. But if it's just cosmetically rough -- leave it. Clean it, oil the fretboard, set it up properly, and let it be what it is. Every ding is a story.

Electric or acoustic for a beginner?

+
Honestly? Electric. I know that's controversial. People think you should "earn" the electric by learning acoustic first. Nonsense. Electric guitars have thinner necks, lower action, lighter strings, and hurt your fingers less. You'll practice MORE because it's more fun. A decent starter pack with guitar, amp, and cable is under 150 quid. But if you want acoustic -- go for it. Just get one with a proper setup. A badly set-up acoustic is the number one reason beginners quit.

What amp should I get?

+
Depends on your budget and where you're playing. For bedroom practice: a Boss Katana Mini (about 60 quid) or a Blackstar Fly 3. For home and small gigs: Boss Katana 50 (the Swiss army knife of amps). For proper gigging on a budget: Fender Blues Junior or a second-hand Marshall DSL40. Don't spend a fortune until you know what sound you want. And for the love of all that is holy -- try before you buy. What sounds great on YouTube sounds completely different in your bedroom.

How do I spot a partscaster?

+
A "partscaster" is a guitar built from parts from different sources -- sometimes genuine, sometimes aftermarket, sometimes a mix. Nothing wrong with them, but you shouldn't pay Fender prices for one. Check: Does the neck date match the body? (Remove the neck on bolt-ons -- dates are in the heel pocket). Are the pickups original to the model? Is the hardware consistent? Look at the neck pocket -- aftermarket bodies often have sloppy routing or shims. Check the potentiometer date codes (on the back of the pots inside the cavity). If the neck says 1965 but the pots say 2015, you've got a partscaster. Not a bad thing -- just price it right.

🎸 The Workshop Code -- Community Rules

+

1. Respect the instrument. Every guitar has a story worth hearing.

2. No snobbery. A well-played Squier beats a dusty Custom Shop every time.

3. Respect each other. No gatekeeping. Every expert was once a beginner.

4. Share knowledge freely. Hoarding information helps nobody.

5. Buy honest. If you're selling, disclose everything. No surprises.

6. Preserve, don't destroy. Think twice before modifying vintage instruments.

7. Welcome the weird. Neurodivergent, odd hours, strange questions -- all welcome. The workbench doesn't judge and neither do we.

🧠 Got a Noisy Brain?

ADHD? Autistic? Both? Welcome home. The founder of the -Oid villages is AuDHD himself. Fred's got ADHD too. Guitar playing and repair is one of the best things for a noisy brain -- repetitive, tactile, absorbing, no small talk required. The workbench doesn't care about your diagnosis. Neither do we. You belong here exactly as you are.

-- Hyperfocus on learning scales? That's a superpower here.

-- Can't sit still? Learn to play standing up -- it's how the pros do it.

-- Sensory overload from crowds? Bedroom recording. Just you, the guitar, and a looper pedal.

-- Need routine? Daily practice is the most rewarding routine you'll ever build.
